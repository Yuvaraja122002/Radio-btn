7.1 ADMINISTERING OXYGEN THERAPY VIA VARIOUS DEVICES

Learning Objective

Students will be able to demonstrate the administration of oxygen therapy correctly.

Definitions

Oxygen therapy: the administration of oxygen at concentrations greater than that of ambient air, with the intention of treating or preventing the symptoms and manifestations of hypoxia.

Hypoxia: a state whereby insufficient oxygen is available in the tissue to meet the requirement of an organ or tissue.

Hypoxaemia: a decrease of oxygen content in the blood.

Hypercapnia: greater than normal amount of CO2 in the blood.

Purpose of Oxygen Therapy

i. To relieve hypoxia.

ii. To reverse hypoxaemia.

Indications of Oxygen Therapy

1. Alteration in ventilation: ventilation depends on patent airways, the ability to clear airways, chest expansion and compliant alveoli. Many factors and disease processes can interfere with adequate ventilation:

  • Limited ability to clear airways: ineffective cough, decreased level of consciousness.
  • Limited alveolar expansion: pneumonia, congestive heart failure, fractured ribs, pain, immobility.
  • Factors that interfere with surfactant activity which is essential for alveolar inflation: narcotics, hypoventilation.

2. Alteration in diffusion: diffusion is dependent on a partial pressure difference in gases and an adequate amount of permeable alveolar surface area. Any disease that thickens the alveolar membrane (fibrosis, congestive heart failure) reduces alveolar permeability and leads to hypoxaemia.

3. Alteration in perfusion: for cells to receive oxygen and give off their waste, blood must flow at both the pulmonary and tissue capillary levels. Adequate perfusion depends on a normal blood volume, an adequate amount of haemoglobin capable of combining oxygen and carbon dioxide, effective cardiac function and competent vasculature. Any pathology that interferes with blood cell production, maintenance of blood volume, cardiac function or vascular patency can result in inadequate tissue perfusion.

Types of Oxygen Device

- Cannula: Nasal Cannula (Prong)

- Face Masks:

i. Simple Face Mask

ii. Venturi Mask with specifically controlled oxygen concentration

iii. Partial Rebreather Mask with Reservoir Bag

iv. Non-rebreather Mask with Reservoir Bag

1.Nasal Cannula (Prong) (Figure 7.1a)

  1. Delivers 24-44 per cent FiO2 (fraction of inspired oxygen) at 1-6 L/min (LPM) flow.
  2. Delivers 4 per cent oxygen per litre flow
    • Flow 1 LPM: 24 per cent
    • Flow 2 LPM: 28 per cent
    • Flow 3 LPM: 32 per cent
    • Flow 4 LPM: 36 per cent
    • Flow 5 LPM: 40 per cent
    • Flow 6 LPM: 44 per cent

Figure 7.1a Nasal prong.

Note:

  • Humidification of low-flow oxygen <4 LPM through nasal cannula is not considered because it supports bacterial growth in a moist environment.
  • Flow rates at ≥4 LPM will irritate the nasopharynx. Use of humidifier will prevent drying of mucous membrane.
  • Rates >6 LPM are not efficacious as there is the tendency for patient to swallow air; it can dry and irritate the mucous membrane.

Cautions:

  • Poorly humidified and high gas flow can cause nasal mucosal irritation and nosebleed.
  • Prolonged use of nasal cannula can cause nasal trauma and skin damage to the cheeks and ears.

2. Face Masks:

i. Simple Face Mask (Figure 7.1b)

  • Delivers 40-60 per cent oxygen at 5-8 LPM flow rates.
  • Room air entrained during inspiration.

Range:

5-6 LPM: 40 per cent

6-7 LPM: 50 per cent

7-8 LPM: 60 per cent

Figure 7.1b Simple face mask.

Note: A minimum of 5 LPM oxygen flow rate is required to flush the exhaled carbon dioxide (CO2) from the patient’s mask to prevent the patient from rebreathing the CO2.
